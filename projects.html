Undergraduate Thesis - July 2018 to November 2018
Magnetometry using Nitrogen-Vacancies in Diamond

I worked on designing the experimental setup for sensing weak magnetic fields at micron-scale resolution using nitrogen-vacancy(NV) centers in diamonds, under the guidance of Prof. Pradeep Sarin and Prof. Kasturi Saha at IITB. We plan on using this as a tool to map propagating electric impulses in neuron cells.
Implanting nitrogen atoms in the diamond lattice lead to a point defect, and together with the adjacent vacancy in the crystal, it forms a two-level quantum system. Rotations can be perfomed on the corresponding Bloch sphere using a microwave excitation. Making the RF field more uniform by replacing the current loops used perviously in the group with a microstrip antenna, I am able to use 57 percent more nitrogen-vacancies, corresponding to an expected shot-noise limited sensitivity improvement of 21 percent. Report

Research Internship, Tyndall National Institute, Ireland - May to July 2018

I worked on developing machine learning techniques to extend the reach of Intensity Modulated/Direct detection photonic communication channels, under the guidance of Dr Cleitus Antony and Dr Paul Townsend. I implemented feed forward neural networks, recurrent neural networks and a deep convolutional neural network at the receiver, and validated their performance against simulations and experiment. Using the convolutional network, I was able to demonstrate 20Gbps transmission across 30Km fibre, over hardware designed for 10Gbps.
I also developed tools to intuitively visualise the weights learned by the neural network to gain insight into its working (Neural networks are typically treated as 'black boxes', since it's usually hard to make sense out of its parameters by simple observation). In addition, I started the work on using neural networks for pre-compensation within the group. You can find the report here.

Research Internship, KEK: High Energy Accelerator Research Organisation, Japan - November to December 2017

Molecular Thermoelectrics using NEGF formalism
Course project, EE755: Quantum transport in nanoscale devices

Using Non-equilibrium Green's Function formalism, I modeled the charge transport phenomena in Phenyl dithiol molecule with focus on its thermoelectric properties. The transport dynamics is determined by the position of the Fermi level with respect to the molecular energy levels - Comparing the inferences from the model with experimental evidence from literature, I concluded that the charge transport is dominated by the highest occupied molecular orbital, and that the charge carriers are primarily holes. Poster. Report

Particle discrimination using Machine learning and matched filtering
Course project, EE338: Digital Signal Processing

We compared machine learning and matched filtering for distinguishing between electrons and pions from the signal of a scintillation detector. We proposed a theoretical model of the signal for the case of electron and pion. The model matched very well with available data. This model was used to generate a training sample to train the regression classifier, and also as the reference for the matched filter. Both classifiers gave over 99 percent accuracy under reasonable noise conditions. Poster

Training Probabilistic Spiking Neuron Networks with First-to-spike Decoding
Course project, EE746:Neuromorphic Engineering

Neurons in the human brain communicate by means of sparse spiking processes. Most existing algorithms of practical implementation of SNNs are based on variations of the unsupervised mechanism of Spike-Timing Dependent Plasticity (STDP), which updates synaptic weights based on local input and output spikes. We implemented a first-to-spike decoding rule, whereby the SNN can perform an early classification decision once a spike firing is detected at an output neuron. This generally reduces decision latency and complexity during the inference phase. Poster. Report

Forgery detection of images using statistical analysis
Course project, EE325: Probability and Random Processes

We extracted several statistical features from test images to detect copy-move forgery. We performed a fourier transform of the suspicious parts of the image, and compared the statistical moments in the fourier domain. Report. Presentation

Matsya, autonomous underwater vehicle - october 2015 to October 2016

Matsya is an AUV designed and developed by a 30 member team of undergraduates spanning various branches of engineering like Electrical, Mechanical, Software and Materials science, at IIT Bombay. The team competes annually at the international RoboSub competition conducted by the AUVSI Foundation, San Diego, California, where we came second internationally last year.
My major contributions to the team include the DC-DC boost converter for powering up the thrusters and the motor driver module which cost around 20 percent of the market alternative, while at the same time providing twice the capacity. I have implemented hot swapping of batteries, allowing drained batteries to be replaced without switching off the on-board computer and at the same time providing an additional layer of safety in case of primary battery failure.

Simulation of spiral RF inductors - April 2016 to June 2016

I worked under Prof. Dipankar Saha, IIT Bombay, on the simulation and analysis of spiral RF inductors in the micron scale, using MATLAB and COMSOL Multiphysics. The major goals included finding a correlation between the trace thickness, width, number of turns and inductance value.
The results of simulation and experiment were found to agree very well for the test cases. I managed to isolate the major cause of deviation from ideal behaviour for the studied models to the current leaking through the substrate into the ground plane. I also roughly estimated the frequency at which the parasitic capacitance becomes dominant. I also tried out slightly modified models, which were found to have better characteristics than the original ones.
